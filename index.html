The Berry Ratio in Transfer Pricing: Concept, Logic and Practical Use

In transfer pricing analysis, transactions between associated enterprises must satisfy the Arm's Length Principle (ALP). To evaluate whether pricing between related parties mirrors what independent parties would agree upon, practitioners employ financial metrics known as Profit Level Indicators (PLIs).

The Berry Ratio is one such PLI, commonly applied in cases involving distribution or intermediary functions. It measures the relationship between gross profit and operating expenses to assess whether a limited-risk distributor earns a return proportionate to the services it provides.

Berry Ratio = Gross Profit ÷ Operating Expenses

The concept was developed from the landmark U.S. case E.I. DuPont de Nemours & Co. v. United States, where Charles Berry first articulated the ratio. It has since gained recognition under OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines and U.S. Treasury Regulations, and is particularly applicable where operating expenses are the primary driver of value creation — such as in limited-risk distributors, commission agents, and service providers.
