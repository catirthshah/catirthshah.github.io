The Berry Ratio in Transfer Pricing: Concept, Economic Logic and Jurisprudential Development

In transfer pricing analysis transactions between associated enterprises must comply with the Arm’s Length Principle. Various transfer pricing methods are used to determine whether related-party pricing reflects market conditions.

The Berry Ratio is a financial indicator sometimes used as a Profit Level Indicator in cases involving distribution or intermediary functions. The ratio evaluates the relationship between gross profit and operating expenses to determine whether a distributor earns an appropriate return for the services performed.

Berry Ratio = Gross Profit / Operating Expenses

The concept originated in the landmark case of E.I. DuPont de Nemours & Co. v. United States where an expert analysis examined the profitability of a Swiss distributor by analysing the relationship between its gross profit and operating expenses.

The ratio is particularly useful in analysing limited-risk distributors because operating expenses typically represent the primary driver of value-creating activities such as marketing, sales and distribution.

However, the reliability of the Berry Ratio depends on proper classification between cost of goods sold and operating expenses as well as comparability between the tested party and independent companies.
