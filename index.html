Transfer Pricing 2025

The Berry Ratio in Transfer Pricing: Concept, Logic and Practical Use

In transfer pricing analysis, transactions between associated enterprises must satisfy the Arm's Length Principle (ALP). To evaluate whether the pricing between related parties reflects what independent enterprises would agree upon, tax authorities and practitioners employ various transfer pricing methods and financial metrics known as Profit Level Indicators (PLIs).

The Berry Ratio is one such PLI, commonly applied where distribution or intermediary functions are involved. It measures the relationship between gross profit and operating expenses to evaluate whether a limited-risk distributor earns a return proportionate to the services it renders.

Berry Ratio = Gross Profit ÷ Operating Expenses

The concept was developed from the landmark U.S. Transfer Pricing case E.I. DuPont de Nemours & Co. v. United States, where Charles Berry first articulated the ratio. It has since gained recognition under the OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines and the U.S. Treasury Regulations as a legitimate and reliable PLI in cases involving limited-risk distributors and commission agents where operating expenses are the primary driver of value creation.

Unlike return on sales or return on assets, the Berry Ratio is particularly appropriate when a tested party does not own significant assets but incurs substantial operating expenses in performing its functions — making it a valuable tool in the modern transfer pricing toolkit.
